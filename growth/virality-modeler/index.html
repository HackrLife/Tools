Theory · Formulas · Benchmarks

What is the K-Factor and why does it matter?

The K-Factor tells you whether your product is growing on its own energy or being kept alive by paid spend. It is the single most important number in viral growth — and one of the most misunderstood. This page explains where it comes from, what the formula means, and how to read the numbers in practice.

Origin

Where K-Factor comes from

K was borrowed from epidemiology — specifically R₀, the reproduction number that describes how many new people each infected person infects. Above 1.0 the disease spreads exponentially. Below 1.0 it dies out.

Early viral game developers noticed user growth followed the same logic. Popularised by Andrew Chen (a16z) and David Skok (Matrix Partners), it is now standard vocabulary in every growth conversation.

The Formula

How this tool calculates K

Two loops combined, scaled by two qualitative multipliers:

K_social = s × r × ctr × c
sShare Rate — fraction who post publicly
rReach — avg. impressions per post
ctrClick-Through Rate — fraction who click
cConversion — fraction of clicks who sign up
K_network = i × c_inv
iInvites per user — direct messages sent
c_invInvite conversion — fraction who sign up
K_total = (K_social + K_network) × (Density × Resonance)
Growth(T) = N₀ × K_total ^ (T ÷ VCT)

Assumes constant loop rates and no market saturation. Treat all outputs as directional estimates.

Interpretation

What does your K score mean?

K < 0.5
Critically Low
Organic acquisition is negligible. Every user is the result of paid effort. Fix the loop before scaling spend.
0.5 – 0.99
Partial Assist
The loop supplements paid but cannot sustain growth alone. Still meaningful — at K=0.8, every 100 paid generates 400 organic over time.
K ≥ 1.0
Self-Sustaining
Growth compounds without additional paid spend. In practice K decays as audience saturates — rarely holds above 1.2 at scale.

The organic multiplier: Organic users per 100 paid = K ÷ (1 − K) × 100. At K=0.5 that is 100 organic. At K=0.8 it is 400. Even a non-self-sustaining K dramatically improves CAC efficiency.

Plan vs Diagnose

How the two tabs work together

Plan (before you post) — you set the K you want and enter your best estimates for share rate, reach, CTR, conversion, and invites. The tool works backwards: given your K target, here is what each metric needs to be. It shows the gap between your estimates and the target so you know which lever to pull before you write a single word.

Diagnose (after you post) — you enter what actually happened: real impressions, real clicks, real signups, real shares. The tool back-calculates your actual K from this post, uses the channel lifecycle window to extrapolate what you will land at by end of distribution, and tells you the single biggest thing to change on the next post. Over time this becomes a feedback loop — Plan, Post, Diagnose, improve, repeat.

Glossary

All terms defined

K-FactorAverage new users recruited per existing user across all loops in one viral cycle
K_socialK contribution from the social feed broadcast loop (public sharing)
K_networkK contribution from the direct invite loop (email, DM, referral links)
VCTViral Cycle Time — days from share or invite to new user activation
Share RateFraction of active users who create a public post featuring the product
CTRClick-Through Rate — fraction of impressions who click through to your page
ConversionFraction of page visitors who complete signup or activation
DensityMultiplier for how well your sharing audience overlaps with your target market
ResonanceMultiplier for content urgency and emotional pull
Leaky BucketK < 1.0 state — paid spend required to maintain growth momentum
LifecycleThe window during which a post continues to receive meaningful organic distribution
